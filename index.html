Module 01

UX Research & Synthesis

Research is the foundation of good design — but it's also the most time-consuming phase. Claude doesn't replace the research; it accelerates the scaffolding and synthesis work so you can spend more time where it counts: listening to real users.

Drafting interview guides

Writing a good interview guide takes experience — you need the right warm-up, open-ended questions that don't lead, and a close that surfaces anything you missed. Claude can draft a strong starting structure in seconds.

Prompt Patterns
Interview Guide Generator
I'm designing [brief product description] for [target user]. I'm running 45-minute user interviews to understand [research goal]. Write an interview guide with: - A 3-question warm-up to build rapport - 6–8 main questions that explore [specific behaviour or pain point] - 2 closing questions to capture anything I may have missed Use open-ended language throughout. Avoid leading questions.
Why this works: Giving Claude the product context, user type, and a specific goal means the questions are tailored — not generic. The structure constraint mirrors how experienced researchers actually run sessions.
Follow-up Question Generator
Here's a key question from my interview guide: "[Your question]" For each of these possible participant responses, write 2–3 natural follow-up questions: - A very brief or surface-level answer - An emotional or personal answer - A practical / process-focused answer Keep all follow-ups open-ended and non-leading.
Why this works: Preparing for different response types means you won't lose momentum mid-interview. Especially useful for less experienced researchers who find improvised follow-ups difficult under pressure.
Watch Out For
⚠️Leading questions: Even when asked to avoid them, Claude occasionally writes questions with assumptions baked in. Read each question critically before using it in a real session.
⚠️Domain specificity: Claude doesn't know your industry's norms or your users' vocabulary. Review every question to make sure it sounds natural — not like a textbook.

Jobs To Be Done (JTBD)

Jobs To Be Done reframes user research around the underlying motivation behind a behaviour — not what people do, but what progress they're trying to make. Claude is a strong partner for JTBD work: generating job statements from research, identifying competing solutions, and helping you map the forces that drive and resist behaviour change.

Prompt Patterns
Job Statement Generator
Here are observations from [N] user interviews: [paste notes or quotes] Using the Jobs To Be Done framework, analyse this research and produce: 1. The core functional job — what are users ultimately trying to accomplish? Write it as: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]" 2. 3–5 emotional jobs — how do users want to feel (or avoid feeling) during and after the job? 3. 3–5 social jobs — how do users want to be perceived by others? 4. The competing solutions users currently hire to get this job done — include non-obvious substitutes 5. What does "done" look like? How do users currently judge whether the job was successful?
Why this works: Breaking the job into functional, emotional, and social layers reveals product opportunities that a purely functional analysis misses. The "competing solutions" step is the most strategically valuable — it shows you who you're actually competing with, which is often not who you think.
Forces of Progress Diagram
Context: [describe your user and the behaviour change you're designing for] Core job: [your job statement from the previous prompt] Map the Four Forces of Progress for this job: 1. Push — what frustrations or limitations with the current situation are driving users to look for something new? 2. Pull — what attracts users toward a new solution? What does progress look like to them? 3. Anxiety — what concerns or uncertainties are stopping users from switching or adopting? 4. Habit — what existing behaviours, routines, or attachments are pulling users back to the old way? For each force, list 3–5 specific examples grounded in the research context above. Then identify: which force is currently strongest, and what design decisions would address it directly?
Why this works: The four forces map shows you where the real friction is — not just what users want, but what's stopping them from getting there. Designing to reduce anxiety or break habits is often more impactful than improving the product's pull alone.
Opportunity Sizing from Jobs
Core job: [your job statement] Current solutions users hire: [list from previous analysis] Using Outcome-Driven Innovation logic, help me identify underserved outcomes: 1. List 8–12 desired outcomes users have when trying to complete this job — write each as: "Minimise/maximise [metric] when [situation]" 2. For each outcome, rate: how important is it (1–5) and how satisfied are users with current solutions (1–5) 3. Flag the outcomes with high importance and low satisfaction — these are the underserved opportunities 4. Suggest which 2–3 of these underserved outcomes would most directly translate into product features
Why this works: Outcome-Driven Innovation gives you a quantifiable way to prioritise — you're designing for gaps between what matters and what current solutions deliver. This is how JTBD moves from insight to product strategy.
Watch Out For
⚠️JTBD needs real research input: Without actual user quotes and observations, Claude will generate generic job statements that apply to almost any product. Feed it specific research — the more concrete, the more useful the output.
⚠️Don't mistake the trigger for the job: "I need a way to share files" is a trigger, not a job. The job is the underlying progress the user is trying to make. Push Claude to go one level deeper if the statement sounds like a feature request.

Module 02

UX Strategy & Methodology

Strategy is what separates a designer from a design thinker. It means framing the right problem before jumping to solutions — and connecting design decisions to real business outcomes. Claude is a strong thinking partner in the early, fuzzy stages of a project.

Framing the right problem

The most expensive design mistake is solving the wrong problem beautifully. Claude can help you generate and pressure-test problem statements before you commit to a direction.

Prompt Patterns
Problem Statement Generator
I'm designing for [user type] who [context of their situation]. From my research, the core tension is: [describe the problem briefly]. Write 3 different problem statements using: "[User] needs a way to [goal] because [insight]." Make each one emphasise a different angle: 1. Focused on the emotional friction 2. Focused on the practical barrier 3. Focused on the unmet opportunity
Why this works: Three angles forces you to examine the problem from different lenses — not just validate the first framing that felt right. Having all three in a stakeholder meeting often reveals where everyone's mental models differ.
How Might We Generator
Based on this problem statement: "[Your problem statement]" Generate 10 How Might We questions that open up different design directions. Include: - Some that address the core friction directly - Some that reframe the problem entirely - At least one that challenges an assumption in the problem statement itself Keep them broad enough to inspire multiple solutions, but specific enough to be actionable.
Why this works: The instruction to challenge an assumption is the most valuable part. Claude often surfaces a framing you hadn't considered — which is exactly what the HMW technique is designed to do.
Watch Out For
⚠️Business context gap: Claude doesn't know your client's constraints, politics, or competitive positioning. Strategy that looks right on paper might be unachievable in practice — always filter through what you know about the business.

Writing design principles

Design principles only work if they're specific enough to make decisions with. Claude can help you draft principles grounded in your research and connected to real business goals — not generic platitudes.

Prompt Patterns
Design Principles Generator
I'm designing [product] for [user]. My research surfaced these key needs: [list 3–4]. The key business goals are: [list 2–3]. Write 4–5 design principles that: - Are specific to this product and user — not generic - Could help a designer choose between two conflicting options - Include a "this means we don't…" example for each - Are memorable enough to reference without looking them up
Why this works: The "this means we don't…" instruction separates useful principles from decoration. A principle is only valuable if it rules something out — this prevents the usual vague outputs like "be human" or "prioritise clarity."
Watch Out For
⚠️Generic outputs: Even with good instructions, Claude can drift toward well-worn principles. Push back in a follow-up if the output could apply to any product — ask for a version that's specific to your domain and user.
Market analysis

Market analysis is one of the most time-consuming parts of early strategy — gathering data on trends, user segments, and industry context before you can make informed design decisions. Claude can accelerate the research and synthesis phase significantly, helping you build a picture of the market landscape faster and surface the opportunities most relevant to your product.

Where Claude fits
  • Summarising market trends and contextualising them for a specific product or user segment
  • Identifying underserved user segments from a description of the market landscape
  • Structuring a market sizing exercise — TAM, SAM, SOM — from rough inputs
  • Mapping industry shifts that create design opportunities or risks
  • Writing a market context section for a strategy deck or client brief
Prompt Patterns
Market Landscape Summary
I'm designing a [product type] for [target user] in the [industry] space. Give me a market landscape summary that covers: 1. The 3–4 most significant trends shaping this space right now 2. The main user segments and how their needs differ 3. Where existing solutions are falling short 4. 2–3 design or product opportunities these trends create Frame everything from a product design perspective — I'm using this to inform design decisions, not an investor pitch.
Why this works: The "design perspective" instruction is critical — without it Claude defaults to business or financial framing. Asking for opportunities that trends create is what makes this useful for designers: it connects market context directly to design direction.
Underserved Segment Finder
I'm looking at the market for [product or service category]. The main players are: [list 3–5 competitors]. Based on what you know about this market: 1. Which user segments are most underserved by existing solutions? 2. What specific needs or pain points do these segments have that current products ignore? 3. Which segment would be most strategically valuable to design for first, and why? I'm a designer, not a business analyst — keep the framing practical and user-centred.
Why this works: Asking Claude to rank segments strategically forces it to move beyond listing — it has to weigh need, reach, and feasibility. The "designer not analyst" instruction keeps the output grounded in user needs rather than revenue projections.
Market Context for Strategy Deck
I need to write a market context section for a strategy deck about [product or project]. Audience: [e.g. "a founder and their team" or "a client's product stakeholders"] What they care about: [e.g. "growth opportunity, user pain, competitive differentiation"] Write a 3–4 paragraph market context section that: - Establishes why this market matters now - Describes the user problem at a market level - Identifies the gap this product is positioned to fill - Sets up the design strategy that follows Keep it concise and free of jargon.
Why this works: Framing it as "sets up the design strategy that follows" gives the section a job — it's not just context for context's sake. This makes the writing purposeful and ensures it transitions naturally into the design work rather than sitting as a disconnected preamble.
Full Market Analysis — Master Prompt
You are a strategic business analyst. Run a structured market analysis for the following idea: **Idea:** [DESCRIBE YOUR PRODUCT/SERVICE IDEA IN 2–3 SENTENCES] **Target user:** [WHO IS THIS FOR?] **Revenue model hypothesis:** [HOW YOU THINK YOU'LL MAKE MONEY] **My background/edge:** [RELEVANT SKILLS, EXPERIENCE, OR EXISTING AUDIENCE] **Geography:** [WHERE YOU'RE STARTING / PRIMARY MARKET] --- Produce the analysis in exactly this structure: ## 1. Market Landscape & Opportunity - Market size & growth: cite 2–3 relevant markets with projected size, year range, and CAGR. Cover the broad market and the specific niche. - What this means for [idea name]: interpret the numbers in context of the specific product. - Key drivers: 3–5 forces accelerating this market right now. - Key risks & challenges: 3–5 honest risks specific to this idea (not generic startup risks). ## 2. Product-Market Fit - Target users: 2–3 specific personas with context on why they're the right fit. - Value proposition: for the user and for the business separately. - My unique edge: how the founder's background maps to an unfair advantage. - Fit check: evidence that the problem is real and the audience is active. - Key hypotheses to test: 4–5 open questions that need validation before scaling. ## 3. Revenue Forecast (5-year illustrative) State your assumptions clearly first (reachable market, conversion rate trajectory, price point, additional revenue streams). Use realistic-but-optimistic assumptions. Add a "Notes & caveats" section below the table. ## 4. Go-to-Market & Growth Strategy Structure as numbered phases: 1. MVP / launch strategy 2. Content & community approach 3. Monetisation and upsells 4. Retention & network effects 5. Differentiation (what makes this defensible) ## 5. No generic startup advice. Ground claims in the market data where possible.
Why this works: The persona opener ("You are a strategic business analyst") shifts Claude's register immediately — it stops hedging and writes like an analyst who's done this before. The structured input block means Claude's output is grounded in your actual idea rather than generic assumptions. Generating user flows

User flows are one of the most thinking-heavy early deliverables — mapping every decision point, branch, and edge case before a screen is designed. AI tools can generate a base flow from a brief description in seconds. That base is never finished work, but it gives you something concrete to critique, restructure, and build from rather than starting from a blank canvas.

⚠️
AI creates the base. You create the flow.

Every AI-generated flow is a starting hypothesis, not a finished artefact. It will miss edge cases, assume happy paths, and reflect the AI's generic understanding of your user — not your research. Always review, challenge, and restructure the output before presenting or using it as a design reference.

Core User Flow Generator

Flow Critique & Gap Finder

Identify any steps that assume too much from the user 2. Flag missing branches — places where the user might fail, hesitate, or go back 3. Point out any steps that could be combined or eliminated 4. Suggest where a user is most likely to drop off and why
Why this works: Use this after generating a first draft — whether from AI or your own sketching. Claude reads the flow with fresh eyes and flags the assumptions you've stopped seeing. The "most likely drop-off" question often surfaces the most valuable redesign insight.
Watch Out For
⚠️Happy path bias: Every AI-generated flow defaults to the scenario where everything goes right. The most important design work happens in the branches — error states, hesitation points, and drop-offs — which AI consistently underrepresents. Always add these manually.
⚠️Generic user assumptions: AI generates flows for a "typical user" that doesn't exist. Your user — with their specific mental model, context, and pain points from your research — will behave differently. The flow is a hypothesis to test against your user knowledge, not a conclusion.
⚠️Don't share before reviewing: An AI-generated flow that hasn't been critiqued and modified is not a design artefact — it's a draft. Never present it to stakeholders or hand it to engineers without walking through it yourself first and making it your own.

Design Thinking vs. Double Diamond

Both frameworks are human-centred and iterative. Design Thinking is stage-by-stage with an empathy-first emphasis — each stage has specific tools and methods. The Double Diamond is more explicit about when to diverge and when to converge. Many teams use them together: Design Thinking as the process structure, Double Diamond as the thinking mode at each stage.

Prompt Patterns
Understand — Persona + Hook Canvas
Project: [brief description] Target user: [who you're designing for] What we know so far: [any existing research, assumptions, or context] Help me understand this user before we begin observing: 1. Draft a research persona — demographics, context, goals, frustrations, and current workarounds. Flag which parts are assumptions vs. known. 2. Apply the Hook Canvas: what habit or trigger brings this user to this problem? What is the variable reward they're seeking? What investment have they already made that creates inertia? 3. Frame the JTBD: when they encounter this situation, what progress are they trying to make — functionally, emotionally, and socially? 4. Describe a "future user" — who might this person become in 2 years if the problem is solved well? What would change about their behaviour?
Why this works: Starting with the Hook Canvas and JTBD before observing primes you to notice the right things in research — not just what users do, but the triggers, rewards, and progress motivations that drive behaviour. The future user question connects today's problem to the longer-term design vision.

The empathy map — Says, Does, Thinks, Feels — looks simple until you're trying to sort 600 words of messy dictation into it alone. The work isn't the framework. The work is the classification. And classification is exactly where AI earns its time.

Tool chain: Co-work with your observation file (e.g. obs_P03.txt) → run the prompt below → Claude can output the quadrants directly in chat, or you can paste into Notion, or ask Claude to lay it out in Notion or FigJam.

Read this observation file and sort each moment or observation into an empathy-map quadrant: Says (things the participant said aloud), Does (observable actions), Thinks (inferred mental state — only if the evidence supports the inference), Feels (inferred emotional state — same rule). For each entry, include the original note and a one-line label. If a moment spans two quadrants, include it in both. Flag any moments that don't fit any quadrant — I want to see those separately.
Why this works: The quadrant rules ("only if the evidence supports the inference") stop Claude from over-inferring in Thinks and Feels — which is the most common empathy map mistake. The flagged residuals often surface the most interesting moments: things that don't fit the model are worth a second look. You can also ask Claude to output directly into FigJam as Says/Thinks/Does/Feels frames.
Observe — Empathy Map + AEIOU
User: [persona or user type] Observation/interview notes: [paste quotes, behaviours, and observations] Run two complementary analyses on these observations: Empathy Map (Says / Thinks / Does / Feels): - Quote directly from the research where possible - Highlight the biggest gap between what they say and what they actually think or feel AEIOU Framework: - Activities: what tasks or routines are they engaged in? - Environments: what physical or digital contexts are they operating in? - Interactions: what are they interacting with — people, objects, systems? - Objects: what tools, artefacts, or devices are part of their experience? - Users: who else is involved or affected? Then: what are the 3 most critical assumptions we're still making that this research hasn't confirmed?
Why this works: AEIOU captures the situational context that an empathy map alone misses — the environment and objects around a user often explain behaviours that seem irrational when viewed in isolation. The critical assumptions question closes the observation step by naming what still needs to be tested.
Define Point of View — 360° + HMW
Research synthesis: [paste empathy map and AEIOU output, or key findings] 1. 360° view: examine this problem from every angle — the user, the business, the technology, the environment, and the people around the user. What does each stakeholder most want from a solution? 2. Write a Point of View sentence: "[User] needs [need] because [insight]" — draft 3 versions that each emphasise a different finding from the research 3. Generate 8 How Might We questions from the strongest PoV. Sort them: too narrow / too broad / well-scoped for ideation 4. Select the best-scoped HMW. What assumptions does it still contain — and which should we challenge before ideating?
Why this works: The 360° view surfaces stakeholder tensions that a user-only PoV will miss — solutions that ignore business or technical constraints often fail in delivery, not design. Three PoV versions prevents anchoring on the first framing that sounds plausible.
Ideate — Brainstorm + SCAMPER
HMW question: [your well-scoped HMW from the Define stage] User: [brief description] Structured ideation session: 1. Generate 10 ideas — aim for breadth, not quality. Include at least 2 that seem absurd or impossible. 2. Apply SCAMPER to the most promising idea: Substitute · Combine · Adapt · Modify · Put to other uses · Eliminate · Reverse 3. Gain depth on the top 3 ideas: for each, push one level deeper — what's the underlying mechanism, and what would the experience feel like at the most important moment? 4. Cluster the 10 ideas into themes — what patterns emerge? Name each cluster. 5. Complete a one-line idea communication sheet for the strongest idea: Problem it solves · Core mechanism · User benefit · Biggest risk
Why this works: The clustering step reveals whether your ideation has genuine breadth or is circling the same solution. If all 10 ideas fall into 2 clusters, you haven't diverged enough — generate another round focused specifically on the clusters that are missing.
Develop Prototype — Plan + Boxing
Idea to prototype: [brief description of the concept] Core assumption to test: [the thing this prototype needs to validate or invalidate] Resources available: [time + fidelity constraints, e.g. "half a day, paper only"] 1. Recommend the minimum fidelity prototype format for this assumption — paper sketch, clickable wireframe, service prototype, or something else. Explain why. 2. Apply boxing and shelving: what's in the box (in scope for this prototype), and what's on the shelf (real but excluded from this test)? Be specific. 3. Write the user task scenario — what will you ask the participant to do? 4. Define success criteria before testing: what does a "pass" look like, and what does a "fail" look like? 5. What are the 3–5 specific things you'll observe or measure during the test?
Why this works: Boxing and shelving is the most practical prototyping discipline — it prevents scope creep while keeping the prototype honest. Defining success criteria before testing is what separates learning from confirmation; teams that define "pass" after the session tend to find what they were looking for.
Test — Feedback Grid + Synthesis
Prototype tested: [brief description] Core assumption: [from the prototype step] Test observations: [paste notes — what users did, said, where they got stuck] 1. Feedback-capture grid — organise findings into four quadrants: What worked · What didn't · Questions raised · New ideas surfaced 2. Did the core assumption hold? What's the evidence for and against? 3. What surprised us — behaviours or reactions we didn't predict? 4. Run an experiment grid for the biggest open question: hypothesis → test → metric → result threshold 5. What stage should we return to? (Understand/Observe = we need more research · Define PoV = the problem framing was wrong · Ideate = we need a different direction · Develop Prototype = iterate on this prototype)
Why this works: The feedback-capture grid captures four types of signal in one pass — most test debrief sessions lose the "questions raised" and "new ideas" quadrants entirely. Question 5 — the return-to-stage decision — is what makes Design Thinking genuinely iterative.
Reflect — Retrospective Board
Design Thinking cycle summary: [brief description of what you did, what you learned, and where you landed] Team size: [N people] Time spent: [duration] Run a retrospective on this cycle: 1. What worked well in our process — what should we repeat next cycle? 2. What slowed us down or produced low-quality output — what should we change? 3. What did we learn about the user that we didn't know at the start? 4. What did we learn about our assumptions — which ones held, which were wrong? 5. What is the clearest next step — and which Design Thinking stage does it belong to? 6. One-sentence summary of this cycle to carry into the next: "We set out to [goal]. We learned [insight]. Next we will [action]."
Why this works: The Reflect stage is often dropped under time pressure — and when it is, teams repeat the same process mistakes cycle after cycle. The one-sentence carry-forward in step 6 is the bridge between cycles: it prevents the next iteration from starting cold and forgetting what was just learned.
Watch Out For
⚠️Treating the stages as a linear checklist: Design Thinking is explicitly iterative — Test and Reflect should regularly send you back to earlier stages. If your team moves through every stage once and ships, you're running waterfall with different labels.
⚠️Skipping real observation for desk research: Claude can synthesise and analyse — but it can't replace watching real users in real contexts. The Understand and Observe stages are where the process earns its authority; AI-generated personas without real observation undermine everything that follows.
⚠️Over-investing in prototype fidelity: High-fidelity prototypes make participants reluctant to give honest feedback. The right fidelity is the minimum needed to test the core assumption — use boxing and shelving to keep scope tight.
⚠️Skipping the Reflect stage: Teams under deadline pressure drop the retrospective first. This means process mistakes compound across cycles. Even 20 minutes on the retrospective board pays back in the next iteration.

Two maps, two scopes. A Customer Journey Map (CJM) captures the full relationship between a person and a product or service — across all touchpoints, channels, and over time. A User Journey Map (UJM) zooms into a single task or flow within the product — what a user does, thinks, and feels step by step as they try to accomplish something specific. Both are discovery and alignment tools; Claude can generate the structure from research and help you identify where design effort is most needed.

⚠️UJM granularity mismatch: Too coarse and you miss the friction; too granular and the map becomes unreadable. Aim for the level where each step represents one meaningful user decision or action.
Mental Models

A mental model is the internal model someone uses to quickly understand and interact with things in the real world. Rather than reasoning from scratch each time, users apply models built from previous experience — with physical objects, other products, or familiar conventions. When your design conflicts with those models, confusion follows. When it aligns with them, the product feels intuitive without explanation.

The design goal

By aligning with a user's mental model — and by helping users adopt the intended model where needed — you can create designs that just work, because users intuitively know what to do. The two levers are: align the design to the model, or reinforce the correct model through visual cues, onboarding, and consistent patterns.

The four-step process
1 Discover — use interviews and observation to surface the models users already hold. Ask them to explain their reasoning as they work through tasks. Map the steps, assumptions, and thought processes you find.
2 Align — design within the framework of expected models. This doesn't mean copying convention slavishly — Uber innovated the taxi experience while staying within the mental model of "calling a car". Innovation inside a familiar model works; innovation that breaks the model needs education to succeed.
3 Reinforce — where the correct model differs from what users expect, use skeuomorphism, consistent patterns, and visual cues to guide users toward it. Familiar metaphors (folders, trash cans, buttons) reduce learning time by borrowing from models users already have.
4 Validate — test early with prototypes. Have users think aloud during tasks. Ask what they expect to happen before they interact. Mismatches between expectation and outcome reveal model gaps that can be fixed before they become support tickets.
Prompt Patterns
Discover Mental Models from Research
Here are observations and quotes from user research on [topic/task]: [paste notes or attach document] Help me map the mental models in this research: 1. What do users believe about how this works — even if those beliefs are wrong? 2. What analogies, metaphors, or real-world comparisons do users reach for? (These reveal the model they're borrowing from) 3. What prior experiences — other products, physical objects, familiar conventions — are shaping their expectations? 4. Are there different mental models across different user segments? Describe each. 5. Create a mental model map: for the dominant model, list the steps, assumptions, and thought processes users apply when they interact with [product/feature]
Why this works: Analogies and metaphors are the fastest route to a mental model — people describe unfamiliar things by comparing them to familiar ones. If users reach for the wrong comparison, that's a signal your labelling, IA, or onboarding needs to do more to establish the right frame.
Align & Gap Analysis
User's mental model: [describe what users believe about how this works] Intended model: [describe how the product actually works — structure, logic, terminology] Analyse the alignment between these two models: 1. Where do they match — what existing expectations does our design already satisfy? 2. Where do they diverge — which gaps are most likely to cause confusion or errors? 3. For each major gap: is this a case where we should align the design to the user's model, or reinforce the correct model through UI cues, onboarding, or consistent patterns? 4. What analogies or reference products could we borrow from to make the intended model feel more familiar? 5. What should we test in a prototype to validate whether users can build the correct model quickly?
Why this works: The align vs. reinforce decision in step 3 is the most consequential in IA — there's no universal right answer. It depends on how learnable the correct model is, how often users encounter it, and whether expert users would be penalised by over-simplifying for novices.
Reinforce Through Design
Intended mental model for [product/feature]: [describe the model users need to hold to use this correctly] Current user model (from research): [describe what users currently believe] Gap: [summarise the key mismatch] Suggest specific design decisions that would reinforce the intended model: 1. Visual metaphors or skeuomorphic cues that connect to models users already hold 2. Labelling and terminology changes that match user language 3. Onboarding or empty state copy that establishes the correct frame before first use 4. Consistent interaction patterns that build the model through repetition 5. Progressive disclosure — what can be hidden initially to reduce model complexity for new users?
Why this works: Reinforcement is more durable than explanation. Users won't read tooltips — but they'll quickly infer how something works from visual cues, familiar metaphors, and consistent patterns. This prompt focuses Claude on the design levers that actually change behaviour.
Watch Out For
⚠️Users rarely describe their own mental model directly: It surfaces in what they do, what confuses them, and what language they reach for — not in answers to "how do you think this works?" Observational research and think-aloud protocols produce better data than direct questioning.
⚠️Competing models in the same user: Users often hold multiple conflicting models depending on context — the same person may approach the same task differently when they're in a hurry vs. exploring. Watch for context-dependent behaviour in your research before settling on a single dominant model.
⚠️Don't only design for the novice model: Expert users build accurate models over time. Optimising purely for beginner expectations can make a product feel patronising or inefficient for the people who use it most — design for both where your user base spans experience levels.

Why persistence matters

A default Claude is a stranger. Smart, capable, ready to help — but with no idea who you are, what you're building, or what your conventions are. Every time you open a new conversation, you're starting from zero. Starting from zero means inconsistent output, longer and longer prompts to compensate, and eventually you stop trusting the tool.

Teaching Claude your system is what changes that. Project instructions tell Claude who you are and what you're working on. Skills tell Claude how to do specific jobs. Together, they're what make the difference between "this AI is useful sometimes" and "this AI actually knows how I work."

Claude Skills

Claude Skills let you save custom instructions, tools, and workflows directly into Claude — so instead of re-explaining context every session, you build a reusable assistant tuned to your exact design practice. This module covers what Skills are, how to build them, and the ones most worth having as a designer.

What are Claude Skills?

Skills are saved instruction sets that extend what Claude can do in a session. Instead of pasting the same context prompt every time — your design system tokens, your brand voice guidelines, your preferred critique format — you save it once as a Skill and invoke it when needed. Claude follows it automatically for the rest of the session.

Where Claude fits
  • Saving a design critique framework so Claude always evaluates work the same way
  • Storing your brand voice and tone guidelines so every copy pass stays on-brand
  • Encoding your design system tokens so Claude references them without being told each time
  • Creating a project-specific assistant that knows your stack, your team's conventions, and your current sprint goals
  • Packaging a multi-step workflow — like a research synthesis routine — into a single invocation
Skills vs. just prompting
  • No repeat setup: Context you've saved in a Skill is loaded automatically — you don't need to paste it every time you open a new session
  • Consistent behaviour: The same Skill invoked across different sessions produces consistent output — useful for team workflows where multiple people need Claude to behave the same way
  • Composable: You can run multiple Skills in a session — for example, a critique Skill and a tone-of-voice Skill active at the same time
Watch Out For
⚠️Stale instructions: A Skill is only as good as its last update. If your design system changes, your brand guidelines shift, or your workflow evolves — update the Skill. Outdated instructions produce confidently wrong output.
⚠️Over-constraining Claude: Very rigid Skills can make Claude less useful for edge cases. Leave room for judgement — define the framework, not every decision within it.
Building a Skill

A well-built Skill has three parts: context (what Claude needs to know), behaviour (how Claude should act), and output format (what the response should look like). The more specific and structured your Skill, the more consistently useful it becomes.

Prompt Patterns
Skill Structure Template
## Context [Describe the project, product, or design system this Skill applies to. Include relevant constraints, audience, and goals.] ## Behaviour When I ask you to [task], you should: 1. [Step one — what Claude does first] 2. [Step two — how it processes or evaluates] 3. [Step three — what it produces] Always [non-negotiable rule — e.g. "reference our design token names, not raw hex values"]. Never [thing to avoid — e.g. "suggest adding new components when an existing one covers the use case"]. ## Output Format Respond with: - [Format element 1 — e.g. a numbered list of issues] - [Format element 2 — e.g. a severity rating per issue: low / medium / high] - [Format element 3 — e.g. one suggested fix per issue, max two sentences]
Why this works: Separating context, behaviour, and output format stops Claude from conflating what it knows with what it should do with that knowledge. Each section does one job — this structure is what makes a Skill reusable rather than just a long prompt.
Design System Skill
## Context I work on [product name]. Our design system uses these tokens: - Primary: [token name + value] - Surface: [token name + value] - Typography scale: [token names] - Spacing scale: [token names] - Component library: [list key components] ## Behaviour When I share a design, a prompt, or a component spec: - Reference our token names, not raw values - Flag any suggestion that would require a new token before recommending it - If a component already exists in our library, use it — don't redesign it ## Output Format For any design feedback: issue → token or component reference → suggested fix. For any generated content: use our token names inline, e.g. "set background to surface/primary".
Why this works: This Skill makes Claude a design system enforcer, not just a generalist assistant. Every suggestion comes back anchored to your actual tokens — which is the most common failure mode when using AI for design work without this kind of grounding.
Watch Out For
⚠️Testing before relying on it: Run a few edge case prompts before using a new Skill in production work. Skills behave differently depending on how the instruction is interpreted — a quick test reveals gaps before they cause real problems.
⚠️Too much in one Skill: A Skill that tries to cover every scenario becomes hard to maintain and harder for Claude to follow reliably. Build focused Skills and combine them rather than one monolithic instruction set.
Skills worth building as a designer

These are the Skills that consistently save time across design workflows — not theoretical use cases, but patterns that come up in almost every project. Start with one, use it until it's reliable, then build the next.

Design Critique Skill

Teaches Claude your critique framework — what to evaluate, how to rate severity, and what format to return findings in. Use it before presenting work to catch issues you've stopped seeing after too many hours on the same screen.

Brand Voice Skill

Stores your product's tone principles, vocabulary, and things to avoid. Every UX copy request — microcopy, empty states, onboarding — comes back sounding like your product rather than generic Claude output.

Research Synthesis Skill

Packages your synthesis method — how to cluster themes, what counts as a strong insight vs. an observation, how to write insight statements. Paste raw notes in, get structured output back in a consistent format every time.

Handoff Annotation Skill

Defines how Claude annotates designs for developers — what level of detail, which token names to use, how to describe interactions and states. Produces developer-ready annotations without you writing them from scratch each time.

Accessibility Review Skill

Saves your accessibility standard (WCAG 2.1 AA, or your own internal bar), the checks you want run, and the output format. Run it against any screen description or component spec before it goes to dev.

Watch Out For
⚠️Skills aren't a substitute for judgement: A critique Skill flags issues based on rules you wrote. It won't catch things that only become problems in context — user research findings, product strategy shifts, or stakeholder constraints that aren't in the Skill. Use it as a first pass, not a final check.
⚠️Share Skills with your team carefully: A Skill that works well for your workflow may not fit how a colleague works. Document what each Skill does and what it assumes before sharing — otherwise you get inconsistent outputs and confusion about why.
